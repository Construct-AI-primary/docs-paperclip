# PART I: GROUND-MOUNTED SOLAR PV SYSTEMS

## 1. FOUNDATION DESIGN

### 1.1 Driven Steel Piles (H-Piles / I-Beams)

**Overview:** The most common foundation for utility-scale ground-mounted solar. Steel
W-sections are driven into the ground using impact or vibratory hammers. Typical for
fixed-tilt and single-axis tracker systems.

**Typical Sizes:**
- W6x7 through W8x18 (most common: W6x8.5, W6x9, W6x15)
- Post spacing: 10-20 ft along rack, 15-30 ft between rows
- Embedment depth: 6-12 ft (varies by soil and loading)
- Above-ground stick-up: 2-4 ft (fixed-tilt), 1-3 ft (trackers)

**Allowable Capacities (typical ranges):**
- Compression: 5-20 kips (granular soils), 3-10 kips (cohesive soils)
- Tension (uplift): 3-15 kips (granular), 2-8 kips (cohesive)
- Lateral: 1-5 kips at top of embedment

**Installation:**
- Equipment: Hydraulic impact hammer (Vulcan 06), vibratory hammer (ICE I-8)
- Driving energy: 4,000-8,000 ft-lbs per blow typical
- Rate: 50-100+ piles per day in favorable soils
- Refusal criteria: 50 blows per 6 inches (ASTM D1586 analogy)
- Pre-production testing: 10+ test piles minimum, typically 2% of total

**Standards & Verification:**
- IBC Section 1810 (Driven Deep Foundations)
- ASTM A36 or A572 Grade 50 steel
- Hot-dip galvanized per ASTM A123 (minimum 2.0 mil zinc)
- Static load testing: ASTM D1143 (axial compression)
- Dynamic load testing: ASTM D4945 (PDA testing)
- Pile integrity: minimum 90% of design embedment depth

**Advantages:** Fast installation, proven technology, readily available, good in
granular soils, no concrete curing time.

**Limitations:** Refusal in dense gravel/boulders, corrosion in aggressive soils,
noise/vibration concerns, difficult in hard rock, may require pre-drilling.

**Cost Range:** $30-80 per pile installed (varies by size, depth, soil conditions).

---

### 1.2 Helical Anchors / Screw Piles

**Overview:** Steel shafts with helix plates welded along the length, torqued into
the ground like a screw. Excellent for moderate soils and where minimal disturbance
is desired.

**Typical Sizes:**
- Shaft diameter: 2.875" to 4.5" OD (round shaft), 2.5" to 4" square shaft
- Helix plate diameter: 8" to 14" (single or double helix)
- Helix plate thickness: 3/8" to 1/2"
- Embedment depth: 5-15 ft

**Capacity Correlations:**
- Torque-to-capacity ratio: Kt = 7-20 ft^-1 (typical 10 ft^-1 for round shaft,
  10-15 ft^-1 for square shaft)
- Installation torque: 5,000-15,000 ft-lbs typical
- Example: 10,000 ft-lbs torque x Kt=10 = 100,000 lbs (100 kips) ultimate capacity
- Factor of safety: 2.0 for static loads (per ICC-ES AC358)

**Allowable Capacities:**
- Compression: 5-25 kips (depending on size and soil)
- Tension: 3-20 kips
- Lateral: 1-5 kips (limited by shaft bending capacity)

**Installation:**
- Equipment: Hydraulic torque head on skid-steer or excavator (5,000-20,000 ft-lb capacity)
- Installation rate: 40-80 anchors per day
- Real-time torque monitoring: digital torque indicator required
- Minimum final torque: must achieve design torque at design depth
- Torque verification: each anchor is individually verified during installation

**Standards & Verification:**
- ICC-ES AC358 (Helical Foundation Acceptance Criteria)
- IBC Section 1810.3 (deep foundations)
- ASTM A36/A572 shaft material
- Galvanizing per ASTM A123 or ASTM A153 (helix plates)
- No static load test typically required if torque correlation verified
- Torque calibration at start of each project

**Advantages:** Instant loading (no curing), torque = verification, minimal
disturbance, install in weather, removable/reusable, good in expansive soils.

**Limitations:** Refusal in dense gravel/cobbles, obstructions, shaft bending
limits lateral capacity, corrosion in aggressive soils, requires adequate torque
head equipment.

**Cost Range:** $40-100 per anchor installed.

---

### 1.3 Concrete Piers / Drilled Shafts

**Overview:** Cylindrical concrete columns cast in drilled holes. Used where
driven piles cannot achieve embedment (expansive clays, high water table,
boulders) or where superior lateral resistance is needed.

**Typical Sizes:**
- Diameter: 18" to 36" (24" most common for solar)
- Depth: 6-15 ft (below active zone in expansive soils)
- Concrete: 3,000-4,000 psi compressive strength (28-day)
- Reinforcement: #4 or #5 vertical bars (4-6 per pier), tied with #3 hoops at 12" O.C.
- Clear cover: 3" (earth-formed), 2" (formed)

**Allowable Capacities:**
- Compression: 15-50 kips (end bearing + skin friction)
- Tension: 5-20 kips (skin friction + bell if present)
- Lateral: 5-15 kips (significantly better than driven piles)

**Installation:**
- Equipment: Drill rig (Auger drill, 18-36" diameter)
- Drilling rate: 15-30 piers per day
- Concrete placement: poured or pumped, 3,000-4,000 psi
- Curing: 7-day minimum before rack loading, 28-day for design strength
- Bell-bottom option: 36-48" diameter bell in expansive soils for tension resistance

**Standards & Verification:**
- IBC Section 1810 (if >10 ft deep), Section 1809 (if <10 ft)
- ACI 318 (concrete design), ACI 336 (drilled shafts)
- ASTM C39 (concrete cylinder testing)
- Cross-hole sonic logging for integrity (deep shafts)
- Expansive soil design: IBC 1803.5.3 (requires belled base or embedment below active zone)

**Advantages:** Superior lateral capacity, handles expansive soils, works in
boulders/hard rock (with rock coring), durable in aggressive soils, bell adds
uplift resistance.

**Limitations:** Slow (curing), expensive, weather-dependent concrete placement,
spoil disposal, groundwater management, larger equipment footprint.

**Cost Range:** $150-400 per pier installed (including concrete and rebar).

---

### 1.4 Ballasted Foundations

**Overview:** Concrete blocks or aggregate-filled frames that rely on weight for
resistance against wind uplift and sliding. Used where ground penetration is
prohibited (contaminated sites, brownfields, landfills, capped surfaces) or for
smaller commercial systems.

**Weight Calculations:**
- Wind uplift force: 15-35 psf at 130 mph (Risk Category I)
- Typical ballast weight: 800-3,000 lbs per post (depends on wind zone and exposure)
- Concrete block: 2'x2'x2' = 600 lbs, 3'x3'x3' = 2,025 lbs (150 pcf concrete)
- Ballast factor of safety: 1.5 for uplift, 1.5 for sliding

**Sliding Analysis:**
- Soil-concrete friction coefficient: 0.4-0.6 (granular), 0.3-0.5 (cohesive)
- Sliding resistance = friction coeff x normal force
- Passive resistance may be included if blocks are partially embedded
- Seismic: must check sliding during seismic event (R=1.5 for ballasted systems)

**Standards & Verification:**
- ASCE 7-22 (wind and seismic loads)
- IBC Chapter 18 (soil lateral pressure, bearing)
- Minimum bearing capacity: 1,000 psf for typical soils (verify per geotechnical report)
- Frost heave: blocks may shift on frost-susceptible soils — use geotextile and granular base

**Advantages:** No ground penetration, fast installation, works on contaminated sites,
removable, no specialized equipment.

**Limitations:** Requires flat or near-flat terrain, heavy (shipping/handling), wind
uplift is critical, sliding is critical on sloped sites, snow accumulation reduces
effectiveness, seismic vulnerability, visual impact.

**Cost Range:** $100-300 per block (material + placement), plus foundation pad prep.

---

### 1.5 Ground Screws

**Overview:** Hollow steel tubes with a continuous helical thread welded on the
outside, rotated into the ground. Smaller and lighter than driven piles; used
increasingly for residential, commercial, and smaller utility projects.

**Typical Sizes:**
- Diameter: 3" to 6" OD
- Length: 4-8 ft (extensions available)
- Wall thickness: 0.25"-0.375"
- Helix pitch: 3"-6"

**Torque Requirements:**
- Installation torque: 1,000-5,000 ft-lbs
- Equipment: hydraulic torque motor on mini-excavator or skid-steer
- Rate: 50-100+ per day
- Torque monitoring required for capacity verification

**Allowable Capacities:**
- Compression: 3-10 kips
- Tension: 2-8 kips
- Lateral: 0.5-2 kips (limited by small shaft diameter)

**Advantages:** Fast, minimal disturbance, small equipment, removable, no concrete,
good in clay and sand.

**Limitations:** Not suitable for dense gravel/cobbles, limited lateral capacity,
corrosion in aggressive soils, not as well-established in US codes as driven piles.

**Cost Range:** $25-60 per screw installed.

## 2. SOIL ANALYSIS REQUIREMENTS

### 2.1 Desktop Study (Pre-Investigation)

Before any field work, the following desktop resources should be reviewed:

- **NRCS Web Soil Survey** (websoilsurvey.nrcs.usda.gov) — soil map units,
  engineering properties, depth to bedrock, depth to water table, corrosion potential
- **USGS Topographic Maps** — terrain, drainage, geologic hazards
- **FEMA Flood Insurance Rate Map (FIRM)** — flood zone designation
- **State geologic maps** — formation identification, known hazards
- **Historical aerial photos** — previous land use, fill areas, drainage changes
- **ASTM E1527 Phase I Environmental Site Assessment** — if contamination is suspected

### 2.2 Field Investigation Methods

**Standard Penetration Test (SPT) — ASTM D1586-18:**
- Split-barrel sampler, 140-lb hammer, 30-inch drop
- N-value = blows for last 12 inches of 18-inch drive
- Typical spacing: 100-300 ft grid for utility-scale, closer at critical features
- Depth: 10-30 ft minimum (or refusal), deeper at equipment pads and electrical pads
- Record: N-value, sample recovery, soil classification per ASTM D2487 (USCS)
- Groundwater level measured in each boring at time of drilling and 24 hours after

**Cone Penetration Test (CPT) — ASTM D5778-20:**
- Continuous profiling: tip resistance (qc), sleeve friction (fs), pore pressure (u2)
- Soil Behavior Type (SBT) per Robertson (1990) or Robertson (2010) charts
- Advantages: continuous data, faster than SPT, no sample disturbance
- Limitations: cannot penetrate gravel/boulders, no physical samples
- Typical: supplement SPT borings at 50-100% of boring locations

**Test Pits (where needed):**
- Backhoe or excavator to 10-15 ft depth
- Useful for evaluating boulder content, fill thickness, root zone, shallow bedrock
- ASTM D5434-21 (field description of soil)
- Log visually, photograph, take bag samples

### 2.3 Laboratory Testing Program

**Index Properties (minimum for every stratum):**
- Moisture content (ASTM D2216)
- Atterberg limits: LL, PL, PI (ASTM D4318) — critical for expansive soil assessment
- Grain-size analysis: sieve (ASTM D6913) + hydrometer (ASTM D7928)
- USCS classification (ASTM D2487)
- Specific gravity (ASTM D854)
- Organic content (ASTM D2974) — loss on ignition

**Strength Testing (2-3 per key stratum):**
- Direct shear (ASTM D3080) — for granular soils and fill
- Unconfined compression (ASTM D2166) — for cohesive soils
- Consolidated undrained triaxial (ASTM D4767) — for slope stability and lateral pile analysis

**Corrosion Testing (2-3 per site):**
- pH (ASTM D4972)
- Electrical resistivity (ASTM G57 or G187)
- Sulfate content (ASTM D4327)
- Chloride content (ASTM D512)
- Redox potential

**Corrosion Potential Classification:**
| Parameter | Low | Moderate | High | Very High |
|-----------|-----|----------|------|-----------|
| Resistivity (ohm-cm) | >10,000 | 2,000-10,000 | 1,000-2,000 | <1,000 |
| pH | 6.5-8.0 | 5.0-6.5 or 8.0-9.0 | 4.0-5.0 | <4.0 |
| Sulfates (ppm) | <150 | 150-1,500 | 1,500-10,000 | >10,000 |
| Chlorides (ppm) | <100 | 100-500 | 500-5,000 | >5,000 |

**Expansive Soil Assessment:**
- PI > 15: potentially expansive (IBC 1803.5.3)
- PI > 25: highly expansive — requires special foundation design (belled piers, moisture barrier)
- Free swell (ASTM D4546): >2% requires mitigation
- Potential Vertical Rise (PVR): calculated per local methodology (Texas: PTPL, Colorado: AASHTO)
- PVR >1 inch: special design required; >4 inches: belled drilled shafts below active zone

### 2.4 Geotechnical Report — Minimum Contents

Per IBC 1802.4 and ASTM D420-18:

1. Project description (solar type, panel configuration, loading assumptions)
2. Site description (location, geology, seismic site class per IBC Table 1613.5.2)
3. Field investigation (boring/CPT logs with coordinates and elevations)
4. Laboratory test results (summary tables)
5. Subsurface conditions (stratigraphy, groundwater, anomalies)
6. Seismic site class (A through F, based on Vs30 or N-values)
7. Foundation recommendations:
   - Pile type, embedment depth, allowable capacities (compression, tension, lateral)
   - Predicted pile capacity tables (depth vs. capacity)
   - Installation criteria (driving energy, torque, refusal)
   - Pre-production test pile program
8. Earthwork recommendations (compaction, subgrade prep, fill specs)
9. Corrosion potential and mitigation (coatings, sacrificial steel, cathodic protection)
10. Frost depth and frost heave assessment
11. Expansive soil assessment and mitigation
12. Construction monitoring requirements
13. Limitations

## 3. STRUCTURAL LOADING

### 3.1 Wind Loads (ASCE 7-22)

**Risk Category:**
- Ground-mounted solar (unoccupied): Risk Category I (I_w = 0.87 for wind)
- Rooftop solar on occupied building: inherits building's Risk Category (typically II, I_w = 1.0)
- Solar on essential facility: Risk Category III or IV

**Design Wind Speeds (ASCE 7-22, Vult):**
- Typical range: 105-170 mph (3-sec gust, Risk Category II)
- Convert to Vasd: Vasd = Vult * sqrt(0.87) for Risk Category I
- Coastal hurricane zones: 150-180 mph

**Wind Force on Solar Panels:**
- Fixed-tilt ground mount: MWFRS and C&C per ASCE 7-22 Chapters 27-30
- Single-axis trackers: often analyzed per ASCE 7-22 with site-specific Cp/Cn values
  from wind tunnel testing or CFD analysis
- Typical net pressures: 15-35 psf (uplift and downforce) at 130 mph, Exposure C
- Tracker stow position: 0-degree tilt (horizontal) reduces wind load by 40-60%

**Key Load Cases:**
- Uplift (critical for foundation tension design)
- Downforce (compression on piles)
- Lateral (drag on panel surface and rack structure)
- Torsion (unequal loading on tracker torque tube)

**Exposure Categories:**
- Exposure B: urban/suburban (lower wind)
- Exposure C: open terrain (most solar sites, default)
- Exposure D: flat unobstructed coastal areas

### 3.2 Seismic Loads (ASCE 7-22 / IBC)

**Seismic Design Category (SDC):** Determined from mapped spectral accelerations
(SS, S1), site class, and occupancy.

**Solar-Specific Considerations:**
- R-factor: 1.5-3.0 depending on racking system (lower than buildings — less ductility)
- Importance factor: 1.0 for Risk Category I, 1.25 for RC II
- Most utility-scale solar: SDC A or B (low seismic), lateral forces often < wind
- Seismic is critical in SDC D+ and for ballasted systems (sliding, overturning)
- Liquefaction: required evaluation if site class is F, or soils are loose saturated sand

**Lateral Seismic Force on Racking:**
Fp = (0.4 * ap * Sds * Wp) / (Rp / Ip) * (1 + 2 * z/h) but not less than:
Fp = 0.3 * Sds * Ip * Wp

Where: ap = 1.0-2.5 (component amplification), Rp = 1.5-3.0, Ip = 1.0

### 3.3 Snow Loads (ASCE 7-22)

- Ground snow load (Pg): from ASCE 7 Figure 7.1 or local building department
- Roof snow load (Pf): Pf = 0.7 * Ce * Ct * Is * Pg
- Ce: exposure factor (0.8-1.2), Ct: thermal factor (1.0 for most solar), Is: importance factor
- Sliding snow: panels below sloped surfaces receive sliding drift loads
- Balanced + unbalanced snow: panels must resist both
- Typical: 10-30 psf roof snow in moderate climate zones, 50+ psf in mountain regions
- Snow is typically not a governing load for ground-mount in most regions (wind controls)

### 3.4 Frost Heave

**Frost Depth:**
- IBC Table 1804.4 provides minimum frost depth by jurisdiction
- Range: 0 inches (southern US) to 60+ inches (northern/central US, Canada)
- Frost-susceptible soils: silts (ML, MH), silty clays (CL-ML), some low-plasticity clays

**Adfreeze Forces:**
- Steel pile in frost-susceptible soil: adfreeze stress = 20-60 psi on pile surface
- Can generate uplift forces of 5-20 kips on a W6x15 pile at 5 ft frost depth
- Mitigation: embed pile below frost depth, use belled base, wrap pile with
  slip layer (geotextile + bentonite), or use sleeves

**Design Approach:**
- Minimum embedment: frost depth + 2 ft below frost line
- Or: design pile capacity for adfreeze uplift resistance

### 3.5 Expansive Soils

**Identification:**
- PI > 15: potentially expansive
- PI > 25: highly expansive
- Free swell >2%
- Active zone depth: 5-15 ft (varies by climate and soil)

**Potential Vertical Rise (PVR):**
- PVR < 1": no special design
- PVR 1-4": moisture conditioning, select fill, or deeper embedment
- PVR > 4": belled drilled shafts below active zone, or moisture barrier systems

**Foundation Mitigation:**
- Belled drilled shafts (bell diameter = 2-3x shaft, embed below active zone)
- Moisture barrier (geomembrane around pier, 3-5 ft deep)
- Non-expansive select fill (4-12 inches) beneath slab-on-grade equipment pads
- Pre-swelling: moisture-condition soil before construction

## 4. DRAINAGE AND STORMWATER MANAGEMENT

### 4.1 Hydrology Analysis

**Pre/Post-Development Comparison:**
- Required for NPDES Construction General Permit and most local stormwater ordinances
- Compare peak discharge rates for 2-, 10-, 25-, and 100-year storm events
- Solar sites typically increase runoff (vegetation removed, compaction, impervious panels)
- Most jurisdictions require post-development discharge <= pre-development for each event

**Runoff Coefficients:**
- Pre-development (pasture/grass): C = 0.15-0.35
- Post-development (solar with vegetation): C = 0.25-0.45
- Post-development (solar with gravel): C = 0.55-0.75
- Panel surfaces: effectively C = 0.95 (impervious, but distributed to ground)

**Panel Drip Line Effect:**
- Rain concentrates at the lower edge of tilted panels (drip line)
- Concentrated flow creates erosion channels below panels
- Drip line area receives 2-3x normal rainfall intensity
- Mitigation: vegetated buffer strips, gravel aprons, drip-line trenches, level spreaders

### 4.2 Stormwater Management Practices for Solar

**Vegetated Swales:**
- Trapezoidal channel with grass lining (max velocity 4-5 fps)
- Typical: 2-4 ft bottom width, 2:1-3:1 side slopes
- Designed for 10-year peak flow with 1 ft freeboard for 100-year
- Check dams at 50-100 ft intervals on slopes >3%
- Vegetation: native grasses, maintained at 4-6 inch height

**Detention/Retention Basins:**
- Dry detention: controls peak rate, drains within 24-72 hours
- Wet retention: maintains permanent pool (water quality + peak control)
- Typical sizing: 0.5-2.0 acre-feet per 100 acres of solar site
- Outlet structure: multi-stage weir or orifice for multiple storm frequencies
- Antiseep collars on outlet pipe, emergency overflow spillway

**Filter Strips / Vegetated Buffers:**
- 25-50 ft wide grassed strip along panel rows
- Slows sheet flow, promotes infiltration, removes sediment
- Max slope: 5-8% for effectiveness
- Designed per state-specific criteria (many reference TR-55)

**Permeable Access Roads:**
- Geogrid (Tensar BX1200 or equivalent) + 6-12 inches of clean aggregate
- Provides structural support while allowing infiltration
- Reduces impervious area in post-development calculations
- Typical road width: 12-16 ft (access), 20-24 ft (primary)
- Subgrade preparation: scarify 6 inches, compact to 95% Modified Proctor

**Level Spreaders:**
- Convert concentrated flow to sheet flow at edge of panels
- Perforated pipe or stone trench along contour
- Prevents gully formation at drip line

**Low Impact Development (LID) Approaches:**
- Maintain existing vegetation where possible
- Minimize grading and compaction
- Use native seed mixes for revegetation
- Disconnected impervious area (panels drain to vegetated ground, not storm sewer)
- Bioswales and rain gardens at concentrated discharge points

### 4.3 NPDES Permit Requirements

- **EPA Construction General Permit (CGP)**: required for >1 acre disturbance
- **Notice of Intent (NOI)**: filed before construction begins
- **Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plan (SWPPP)**: required at project site
  - Site description and map
  - Erosion and sediment controls
  - Inspection schedule and log
  - Spill prevention and response
- **Notice of Termination (NOT)**: filed after final stabilization (70% vegetation)
- State-specific permits may be more stringent

### 4.4 Typical Stormwater Management for Utility-Scale Sites (100-500 MW)

| Component | Typical Size/Scope |
|-----------|-------------------|
| SWPPP | Required for all sites >1 acre |
| Detention basins | 2-8 basins per 500 MW site |
| Vegetated swales | Along all access roads and panel rows |
| Filter strips | 25-50 ft wide below drip lines |
| Silt fence | Perimeter of all disturbed areas |
| Level spreaders | At concentrated flow points |
| Permeable roads | All access and maintenance roads |
| Revegetation | Native seed, 70% cover for NOT |
| Inspection frequency | Weekly + after 0.5" rain events |

## 5. SITE PREPARATION

### 5.1 Clearing and Grubbing
- Remove trees, brush, roots >2 inches diameter
- Stump removal: grind or pull (do not leave organic material >3% by weight)
- Stockpile topsoil for later reuse in revegetation
- Haul and dispose of debris per local regulations

### 5.2 Grading
- **Fixed-tilt**: maximum slope 15-20% along rows, 5-10% across
- **Single-axis tracker**: maximum slope 5-8% north-south (tracker axis), 10-15% east-west
- **Top-of-pole mount**: minimal grading needed (point foundations)
- Balance cut/fill on-site: haul distance <1-2 miles for economics
- Subgrade compaction: 95% Modified Proctor (ASTM D1557) for structural areas
- Proof-roll subgrade with loaded dump truck: repair soft areas (yielding >1 inch)
- Finish grade: 1-2% minimum slope for positive drainage

### 5.3 Access Roads
- Primary (perimeter): 20-24 ft wide, 6-12 inch aggregate base, geogrid reinforcement
- Secondary (row access): 12-16 ft wide, 4-8 inch aggregate base
- Turning radii: 40-60 ft (for maintenance trucks, fire access)
- Maximum grade: 8-10%
- All-weather surface: compacted aggregate or geogrid + gravel
- Ditch drainage: V-ditch or swale on at least one side

### 5.4 Erosion and Sediment Control Installation Sequence
1. Perimeter silt fence (before any grading)
2. Construction entrance (stabilized with geotextile + aggregate)
3. Inlet protection (where storm drains exist)
4. Sediment basins (early in grading sequence)
5. Slope stabilization (temporary seed/mulch on slopes >3:1 within 14 days)
6. Dust control (water truck, chemical suppressant)

### 5.5 Environmental Compliance During Construction
- **Environmental monitor** on-site during all ground-disturbing activities
- **Biological monitor** if sensitive species are present
- **Cultural resources**: stop work if artifacts discovered, notify SHPO
- **Wetlands**: flag and fence off prior to construction, 50 ft buffer minimum
- **Noise**: limit construction hours (typically 7am-7pm)
- **Dust**: maintain dust control per air quality permit

## 6. ROOF STRUCTURAL ANALYSIS

### 6.1 Load Inventory

**Dead Loads (existing):**
- Roofing material: 1-5 psf (membrane, metal, tile, shingle)
- Roof deck: 2-6 psf (metal deck, wood sheathing, concrete slab)
- Structural frame: 2-15 psf (joists, beams, trusses)
- Insulation: 0.5-3 psf
- MEP equipment: 1-5 psf
- Typical total existing dead load: 10-30 psf

**Solar Dead Loads (new):**
- PV modules: 2.5-4.0 psf (typical 72-cell module: 55-65 lbs, 6.5 sq ft)
- Racking/mounting: 1.0-3.0 psf
- Ballast (if applicable): 5-15 psf
- Typical total solar dead load: 4-8 psf (mechanically attached), 10-20 psf (ballasted)

**Live Loads:**
- Roof live load per IBC Table 1607.1: 20 psf (reducible for large tributary areas)
- Maintenance/construction live load on panels: not typically added (panels are not walkable)
- Solar load combinations: solar dead load replaces some roof live load per engineering judgment

**Snow Loads (ASCE 7-22, Chapter 7):**
- Ground snow (Pg): from ASCE 7 Figure 7.1 or local building official
- Roof snow (Pf): = 0.7 * Ce * Ct * Is * Pg
- Drifting: at roof steps, parapets, RTU's (triangular drift per ASCE 7-22 7.8)
- Sliding snow: on sloped roofs, panels below receive sliding load
- Solar racking must not create drift conditions that exceed original design

**Wind Loads (ASCE 7-22):**
- Rooftop solar analyzed as Components & Cladding (Chapter 30) and/or
  as rooftop structure (Section 29.4 for rooftop equipment)
- Uplift is the critical concern for ballasted systems
- Wind tunnel testing often required for large arrays or unusual configurations
- Typical net uplift: 20-50 psf at roof edge/corner, 10-25 psf in field of roof
- Parapet effects: parapets can reduce wind uplift on rooftop panels

**Seismic Loads:**
- Fp = (0.4 * ap * Sds * Wp) / (Rp / Ip) per ASCE 7-22 Section 13.3
- Solar weight adds to building seismic mass (IBC requires accounting for this)
- Ballasted systems: seismic sliding check required (Fp > friction resistance)
- Mechanically attached: loads transferred to roof structure and supporting framing

### 6.2 Roof Capacity Assessment

**Key Steps:**
1. Obtain original structural drawings (or perform field survey if unavailable)
2. Inventory all existing loads (dead, live, MEP, future planned loads)
3. Calculate remaining capacity: Available = Design Capacity - Existing Loads
4. Compare solar loads to remaining capacity
5. Check individual member capacity (joists, beams, columns, foundations)
6. Check roof deck capacity (point loads from attachments)
7. Check connection capacity (joist-to-beam, beam-to-column, anchor bolts)

**Typical Roof Capacity Results:**
| Roof Type | Typical Remaining Capacity | Solar Load | Feasibility |
|-----------|---------------------------|------------|-------------|
| Metal deck on joists | 5-15 psf | 4-8 psf | Usually feasible |
| Concrete slab on metal deck | 10-25 psf | 4-8 psf | Almost always feasible |
| Wood frame (residential) | 3-10 psf | 3-5 psf | Often feasible |
| TPO/PVC on lightweight concrete | 2-8 psf | 4-8 psf | May require ballast reduction |
| Built-up roof on wood | 3-8 psf | 4-8 psf | Marginal, check carefully |

**Red Flags (likely infeasible without reinforcement):**
- Lightweight insulating concrete roof decks (LWIC)
- Wood roof decks with deteriorated sheathing
- Pre-engineered metal buildings with thin roof panels
- Roofs already at or near design capacity
- Roofs with large MEP installations already present

### 6.3 Roof Type Considerations

**Flat Roofs (Commercial/Industrial):**
- Ballasted systems common (no penetrations)
- Tilt: 5-15 degrees typical (ballast increases with tilt)
- Attachment spacing: 4-6 ft OC along rack, 3-5 ft across
- Structural check: roof deck capacity at attachment points, drift load zones
- Waterproofing: flash all penetrations per roof manufacturer specs

**Sloped Roofs (Residential/Commercial):**
- Mechanically attached (rail or rail-less systems)
- Flush-mount or low-profile tilt (5-15 degrees)
- Attachment to rafters/trusses (not just sheathing)
- Snow sliding: panels can increase sliding load on lower roof areas
- Wind: suction on leeward side increases with tilt

**Standing Seam Metal Roofs:**
- S-5! clamps or similar seam-clamping attachments
- No penetrations (maintains roof warranty)
- Clamp capacity: 500-2,500 lbs per clamp (manufacturer rated)
- Check seam engagement depth and clip spacing

**Membrane Roofs (TPO, PVC, EPDM):**
- Ballasted systems preferred (no penetrations)
- If mechanical attachment: roof manufacturer-approved flashing details
- Warranty concerns: roof manufacturer must approve attachment method
- Rigid insulation: point loads can crush insulation — use bearing plates

## 7. ATTACHMENT METHODS

### 7.1 Ballasted Racking Systems

**Design:**
- Concrete blocks or manufactured ballast trays
- Weight per unit area: 4-15 psf (depends on wind zone, tilt, exposure)
- Friction: neoprene pad or rubber mat between ballast tray and roof membrane
- Friction coefficient: 0.5-0.7 (rubber on TPO), 0.4-0.5 (concrete on membrane)
- Minimum roof slope: 0-5 degrees for ballasted systems (steeper = more ballast)

**Uplift Resistance:**
- Resistance = ballast weight - net wind uplift
- Safety factor: 1.5 for uplift (some jurisdictions require 2.0)
- Edge/corner zones require 1.5-2x the ballast of interior zones
- Seismic: Fp < mu * W_ballast (friction check for sliding)

**Roof Protection:**
- Protective mat (1/4" rubber or HDPE) between ballast tray and membrane
- Slip sheet if required by roof manufacturer
- Vegetation protection: prevent root penetration under ballast trays

**Limitations:**
- Not suitable for roofs with limited capacity
- Wind exposure B only in many manufacturer specs (exposure C requires engineering)
- Not recommended for slopes >5 degrees
- Snow accumulation on ballast trays adds dead load (must account for)

### 7.2 Mechanically Attached Racking

**Penetrating Attachments:**
- Lag bolts into structural members (wood): 5/16" min, 2.5-4" embedment
- Self-tapping screws into metal decking: #14 min, per manufacturer specs
- Structural attachments must engage roof structure (rafters, joists, purlins)
  NOT just roof sheathing or decking

**Flashing and Waterproofing:**
- Step flashing (shingled roofs): integrated with roofing material
- Boot flashing (flat roofs): EPDM or TPO pipe boot per roof manufacturer
- Curb flashing (flat roofs): manufactured curb with membrane wrap
- Sealant: butyl tape, silicone, or manufacturer-specific sealant
- All penetrations must be flashed per NRCA guidelines and roof warranty requirements

**Attachment Spacing:**
- Typical: 4 ft OC along rack (varies by wind zone and racking system)
- Engineering: calculated per ASCE 7-22 wind loads and racking manufacturer specs
- Edge/corner zones: reduced spacing (2-3 ft OC) or additional attachments

**Structural Loading at Attachments:**
- Uplift per attachment: 200-1,500 lbs (depends on tributary area and wind)
- Downforce per attachment: 100-500 lbs
- Lateral per attachment: 50-300 lbs
- Point load on roof deck: check local bending capacity

### 7.3 Hybrid Systems
- Combination of ballast and mechanical attachment
- Common in edge/corner zones: mechanical attachments at perimeters, ballast in interior
- Reduces total roof loading while maintaining wind resistance
- Engineering: sequential load path analysis required

- Ridge: 3 ft (sloped roof, both sides)
- Eave: 1-2 ft

- Skylights: 3 ft
- HVAC/RTU: 3 ft

## 9. WATERPROOFING AND ROOF INTEGRITY

### 9.1 Penetration Management

**Critical Rule:** Every roof penetration is a potential leak. Minimize penetrations
and flash every one properly.

**Flashing Types by Roof System:**
| Roof System | Flashing Method | Key Detail |
|-------------|----------------|------------|
| TPO/PVC membrane | TPO-boot or curb with membrane wrap | Heat-welded or adhesive |
| EPDM membrane | EPDM boot or curb with EPDM wrap | Adhesive-bonded |
| Modified bitumen | Pitch pocket or curb + mastic | Multi-ply wrap with mastic |
| Standing seam metal | S-5! clamp (no penetration) | Seam-clamping |
| Exposed fastener metal | Pipe boot with metal backpan | Sealant + boot |
| Asphalt shingle | Step flashing (integrated) | Weave with shingles |
| Clay/concrete tile | Flashing under tile + weatherblock | Tile replacement or cutting |

### 9.2 Roof Warranty Considerations

- Most commercial roof warranties are 10-20 years
- Penetrations may void warranty unless approved by roof manufacturer
- Obtain **roof manufacturer approval letter** before installation
- Use only roof manufacturer-approved flashing details
- Some manufacturers require their own contractors for penetrations
- Document all penetrations with photos for warranty records

### 9.3 Long-Term Roof Performance

- **Drainage:** panels must not impede roof drainage (ponding water)
- **Inspection access:** leave pathways for roof inspection and maintenance
- **Thermal cycling:** attachment points experience thermal cycling stress
- **Roof life vs. solar life:** roof replacement during solar system life is costly
  (typically recommend minimum 10 years remaining roof life before installing solar)
- **Ballast migration:** ballast blocks can shift over time; periodic inspection needed
- **Vegetation:** shade from panels may promote moss/algae growth on some roof types

## 10. SITE PREPARATION CONSTRUCTION SEQUENCE

### Phase 1: Pre-Construction (Months 1-6)
1. Land acquisition/lease (ground-mount) or building owner agreement (rooftop)
2. Topographic survey / roof survey
3. Environmental assessments (wetlands, ESA, cultural resources)
4. Geotechnical investigation (ground-mount) or structural assessment (rooftop)
5. Geotechnical/structural report
6. Permitting (building, electrical, stormwater, environmental)

### Phase 2: Site Preparation (Months 4-10)
1. Clear and grub (ground-mount) or roof preparation (rooftop)
2. Access road construction (ground-mount)
3. Grading and drainage installation (ground-mount)
4. Erosion/sediment control installation (ground-mount)
5. Staging area preparation

### Phase 3: Foundation/Structural Installation (Months 8-14)
1. Pre-production pile testing program (2% of piles, load test to 2x design)
2. Production pile/anchor installation with monitoring
3. Pier drilling and concrete placement (if applicable)
4. Ballast placement (if applicable)
5. Attachment installation and flashing (rooftop)

### Phase 4: Racking and Module Installation (Months 12-20)
1. Racking/tracker assembly and attachment to foundations
2. Module installation
3. Electrical wiring and connections
4. Equipment pad foundations (inverters, transformers, switchgear)

### Phase 5: Commissioning and Closeout (Months 18-24)
1. System commissioning and testing
2. Final grading and seeding (ground-mount)
3. Erosion control removal after 70% vegetation establishment
4. As-built surveys and documentation
5. O&M manual delivery
6. Warranty and monitoring system activation

## APPENDIX C: COMMON GEOTECHNICAL FAILURE MODES

| Failure Mode | Mechanism | Soils at Risk | Mitigation |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|------------|
| Pile punch-through | Soft layer below bearing stratum | Soft clay below sand | Deeper embedment, load test |
| Excessive settlement | Consolidation of fill or soft clay | Fill, soft clay | Preloading, surcharge, deeper piles |
| Uplift failure | Expansive clay shrink-swell | High PI clays | Belled piers below active zone |
| Lateral spreading | Seismic liquefaction | Loose saturated sand | Densification, ground improvement |
| Frost heave | Ice lens formation in frost-susceptible soil | Silts, silty clays | Embed below frost depth, slip layers |
| Corrosion | Steel loss in aggressive soils | Low pH, high sulfates/chlorides | Galvanizing, coatings, sacrificial steel |
| Scour/erosion | Concentrated runoff at drip line | Erodible soils, steep slopes | Rip-rap, vegetation, level spreaders |
| Slope failure | Gravity + water on slopes | Steep sites, weak soils | Reduce slope, drainage, retaining walls |
| Pile refusal | Boulders, dense gravel, bedrock | Glacial till, alluvial deposits | Pre-drilling, alternate foundation type |
